Act IThe brief

Every city has a lineup. Nobody printed it.

Hiring a guitarist for a birthday, a comedian for an office party, or a painter for a Saturday workshop is a solved problem — if you happen to know someone. For everyone else, local talent hides in Instagram DMs, flyers, and friend-of-a-friend texts. Localites set out to give that talent a marquee: a two-sided marketplace where guests browse performers the way they'd browse a menu, and performers run their side hustle like a business.

Discovery is word of mouth

There was no place to browse performers. Finding one meant already knowing they exist — a cold-start problem for guests and a ceiling for talent.

Pricing is a negotiation

Nobody published rates, so every booking began with an awkward "so… what do you charge?" Opaque pricing kept casual bookings from ever happening.

Two sides, zero tooling

Performers juggled calendars, chats and payments across four apps. A marketplace only works if the supply side wants to live in it too.

The bet: if browsing performers feels like reading a lineup and prices read like a menu, booking live talent becomes an impulse — not a project.

Act IIThe system

One brand, two spotlights.

The identity had to work twice as hard as a normal consumer brand: energetic enough to sell a night out, sober enough that a performer trusts it with their payout. The answer was a strict two-color contract — coral for the show, navy for the business — over a cool gray house that lets photography of real performers carry the page.

Coral owns energy: heroes, growth actions, active states, category tags. Navy owns commitment: log in, browse, pay. Gray and white stay out of the way so performers' photos are the loudest thing on any screen.

The mark

Localites

A performer mid-stride, flag raised — carrying the show to your block, gig bag on their back. The coral reads as motion and applause at once; the rounded wordmark keeps the whole lockup on a first-name basis. The figure survives at 16px, so it doubles as the app icon and the "Tours" tab glyph.

Act IIIThe screens

Browse like a lineup. Book like a menu.

The web landing page skips the marketing-site ritual entirely — no testimonial carousel, no pricing table. It opens as the product itself: one city field, the lineup floating over the fold, and tonight's recommendations already priced.

The city is the query

Supply is hyper-local, so the hero asks exactly one thing. Suggestion chips seed the empty state; dates and budgets belong after you've seen what your city offers.

The lineup floats

All six verticals ride one card floating over the hero seam — the whole catalog is legible before the first scroll, with "Recommended" leading so cold-start guests never land on an empty search.

Price on the card

Hourly rate, rating and a verified badge are card-level information, not a detail-page reveal. The card answers the three questions that decide a tap: who, how good, how much.

On iOS, the same system folds into a thumb: a split front door for guests and performers, then an explore feed that treats categories as places to wander, not filters to configure.

iOS · Explore

Two doors, one app

"Switch to Host Side" sits on the very first screen, carrying the brand mark. Performers aren't a settings-menu afterthought — the supply side gets a front door, which is what makes a two-sided marketplace two-sided.

Chips before modals

"Tonight" is pre-selected — the most common intent is the default, not a form field. Filters and sorting stay one thumb-tap away without a full-screen sheet.

Tours in the tab bar

Multi-stop experiences earned top-level navigation — marked with the brand's own runner. The bet: packaged nights out become the product's signature order, like a tasting menu.

Act IVCurtain call

What shipped, and what I'd play next.

A working two-sided MVP

Guest booking and the performer's Host Side shipped together on iOS, with the responsive web experience opening straight into the live product.

A brand that scales down

The runner mark holds up from the landing-page hero to a 21px tab-bar icon, and the coral/navy contract kept every new screen decidable without a debate.

Pricing as the trust device

Publishing hourly rates on every card reframed booking from "hire a vendor" to "order off a menu" — the single decision the rest of the design leans on.

One nav, two audiences

The hardest problem wasn't visual — it was keeping guests and performers in one app without either feeling like a guest in someone else's product. The split entry screen was the cheapest possible answer, and it worked because it was honest.

What I'd ship next

Availability calendars so "book now" can mean now; verified reviews to put weight behind the star row; and packaged Tours as the growth loop — turning single bookings into itineraries.
